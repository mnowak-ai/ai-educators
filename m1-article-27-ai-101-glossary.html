AI 101: Key Terms You Need to Know

Before you can fully understand how AI works in business, school, and everyday life, you need a foundation of the core vocabulary. This is your reference guide — searchable, interactive, and built to stick.

AI comes with its own language — and like any language, the more familiar you are with the vocabulary, the more confidently you can think, talk, and make decisions in that space. Use the search bar or browse by letter to explore every key term.

A
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Core Concept
+
The ability of computers or machines to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence — like solving problems, recognizing speech, understanding language, or making decisions.
a very capable assistant that can learn, adapt, and help with complex tasks — but only does what it's been designed and trained to do.
A
Algorithm
Technical
+
Step-by-step instructions or rules that guide how an AI system processes information, learns, and makes decisions. Algorithms are the "recipes" behind everything AI does.
a recipe — it lists every step in order, and following it correctly produces a predictable result every time.
A
Automation
Core Concept
+
When tasks are completed automatically by a system or machine without direct human input. In school and business, this could include grading quizzes, organizing files, sending reminders, or generating reports.
a dishwasher — you load it and walk away. It handles the repetitive task so you can focus on things that actually need your attention.
B
Bias (in AI)
Ethics
+
When an AI system produces unfair or skewed results because of imbalanced or incomplete training data. Bias in AI can lead to discrimination or inaccurate outcomes, especially for underrepresented groups.
a scale that was calibrated wrong — it gives you a number, but that number doesn't reflect reality accurately.
C
ChatGPT
Tool / Product
+
A conversational AI tool developed by OpenAI that can answer questions, write essays, summarize text, generate code, and much more. It's one of the most widely used examples of generative AI available to the public.
a very knowledgeable (but sometimes overconfident) study partner who can help you think through problems — but needs to be fact-checked.
D
Data
Core Concept
+
Information that AI uses to learn and make decisions. Data can include text, images, numbers, audio, and more. The more high-quality data an AI system has access to, the more accurate and useful it becomes.
the food an AI eats. Feed it good, diverse, nutritious data — and it grows smart. Feed it junk or limited data — and its thinking will reflect that.
G
Generative AI
Core Concept
+
A form of AI that can create new content — text, images, audio, video, or code — based on patterns it has learned from existing data. Tools like ChatGPT and AI image generators are examples of generative AI.
a creative intern who has read everything and can produce a first draft of almost anything — but still needs a human to review, edit, and take responsibility for the output.
H
Hallucination
Ethics
+
When an AI generates information that sounds confident and plausible but is factually incorrect or completely made up. This is a known limitation of language models like ChatGPT — they can invent citations, statistics, or events that don't exist.
a student who didn't study but still writes a full confident-sounding essay — it looks believable until you fact-check it.
L
Large Language Model (LLM)
Technical
+
A type of AI trained on massive amounts of text data that can understand and generate human language. LLMs are the technology behind tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Google Gemini.
someone who has read billions of books, articles, and websites and can now hold a fluent conversation on almost any topic — though they don't actually "understand" things the way humans do.
M
Machine Learning
Technical
+
A type of AI where systems learn from data and improve over time without being explicitly programmed for every situation. The more data the system sees, the better it gets at its task.
a student getting better at a sport through practice — nobody told them every possible move, but they learned from experience and got smarter about it over time.
N
Natural Language Processing (NLP)
Technical
+
The branch of AI focused on helping computers understand, interpret, and generate human language — both spoken and written. NLP powers chatbots, voice assistants, translation tools, and grammar checkers.
a universal translator — it bridges the gap between the way humans naturally speak and write, and the way computers process information.
P
Patterns
Core Concept
+
Trends or repeated relationships in data that AI systems detect and use to make predictions or decisions — for example, spotting grammatical errors in writing, or identifying which students are likely to struggle with a concept.
noticing that every time you eat a certain food you feel tired — once you spot the pattern, you can predict and act on it. AI does this at massive scale.
P
Prompt
Core Concept
+
The input or instruction you give to an AI tool — the question, command, or description that tells it what to do. Writing clear, specific prompts is one of the most important skills for using AI effectively.
the brief you give a contractor. Vague instructions produce vague results. Detailed, specific instructions produce exactly what you need.
T
Training Data
Technical
+
The dataset used to teach an AI system how to perform a task. An AI learns by analyzing patterns in its training data — which means the quality, diversity, and accuracy of that data directly shapes how the AI behaves.
the curriculum a student learns from. If the textbooks are biased, incomplete, or outdated, the student's understanding will reflect that — no matter how hard they studied.

📌 Terms to Remember
AI — machines performing tasks that require human-level thinking, powered by data and algorithms.
Machine Learning — AI that improves through experience with data, not just fixed instructions.
Generative AI — AI that creates new content (text, images, audio) from patterns in training data.
Hallucination — when AI confidently produces false or invented information that sounds real.
Prompt — the instruction you give AI. Better prompts produce better, more useful results.
