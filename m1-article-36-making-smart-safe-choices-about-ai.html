Making Smart, Safe Choices About AI in Education

Adopting AI tools in the classroom shouldn't be about jumping on the latest trend — it's about making informed, ethical decisions that protect students and enhance learning. These eight terms help you evaluate AI tools before you use them.

Risk/Benefit Analysis
+
A risk/benefit analysis is a structured process of weighing the potential advantages of using an AI tool against its possible harms — before adopting it. In education, benefits might include saved time, more personalized feedback, or expanded access to learning support. Risks might include data privacy concerns, bias in outputs, equity gaps in access, or reduction in students' independent skill development. Responsible adoption requires this analysis to happen before — not after — a tool enters the classroom.
Questions to ask before adopting any tool:
What specific learning problem does this tool solve?
What student data does it collect, and what happens to that data?
Who might be disadvantaged by this tool's use — and how significantly?
What would we lose if we don't use this tool?
Complete a written risk/benefit analysis for every significant AI tool before adoption. Share it with colleagues and administrators — and revisit it after the first semester of use.
🔐
Privacy vs. Convenience Trade-Off
+
The privacy vs. convenience trade-off describes the tension between using AI tools that offer efficiency and ease versus protecting students' rights to data privacy. Many AI tools that are free, fast, and simple to use operate by collecting and using student data to train their models or generate revenue — often without users fully understanding this exchange. In education, where students are often minors and may not fully grasp the implications, this trade-off carries extra ethical weight. Convenience is real; so is the cost.
Questions to interrogate this trade-off:
What is this free tool's actual business model — how does it make money?
What student data is collected, and can it be deleted?
Is the convenience worth the privacy cost for the specific students in my classroom?
Treat "free" AI tools with extra scrutiny — if you're not paying for the product, the product is often the data. Require district-approved tools with explicit privacy commitments for any classroom use involving student data.
🗄️
Data Handling
+
Data handling refers to the policies and practices governing how student information is collected, stored, shared, used, and eventually deleted by an AI tool or platform. Strong data handling practices include: clear disclosure of what's collected and why, secure storage with access controls, limits on how long data is retained, no sharing with third parties without explicit consent, and a clear process for deletion on request. Weak data handling — vague privacy policies, indefinite retention, or third-party sharing — puts students at risk long after they've stopped using a tool.
Data handling questions for any AI vendor:
What student data do you collect, and exactly how is it used?
How long do you retain student data after a student stops using the platform?
Is student data used to train your AI models?
What is your process for deleting a student's data on request?
Read the data handling section of every AI tool's privacy policy before adopting it — or ask your district technology officer to do so. If the policy is vague, that's a hard no.
📋
Compliance
+
Compliance in educational AI refers to adhering to federal, state, and local laws and regulations that govern student data privacy and digital tool use in schools. Key laws in the U.S. include FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act), COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act, which applies to students under 13), and various state-level student privacy laws. Compliance isn't just a legal box to check — it's a floor, not a ceiling. A tool can be technically compliant and still be ethically questionable. But non-compliance is a clear disqualifier.
Compliance questions before adoption:
Is this tool FERPA-compliant? COPPA-compliant if used with students under 13?
Has our district signed a data processing agreement with this vendor?
Are there state-specific student privacy laws that apply to this tool?
Never adopt an AI tool for classroom use without verifying district compliance approval. If your district doesn't have a vetting process for EdTech tools, advocate for one — and in the meantime, err on the side of caution.
🔬
Bias and Fairness Testing
+
Bias and fairness testing is the systematic evaluation of an AI tool's outputs across different demographic groups — race, gender, language background, disability status, socioeconomic status — to determine whether the tool produces equitable results for all students. Responsible AI vendors conduct this testing before releasing a product and share the results publicly. Schools should conduct their own equity audits after adoption to verify that fairness holds up in their specific student population, since results can vary significantly across different school contexts.
Bias testing questions for vendors:
Has this tool been tested for bias across different racial, linguistic, and disability groups?
Can you share the results of your equity testing?
How do you monitor for bias after deployment?
After adopting any AI tool, track whether outcomes differ significantly across student groups in your classroom. If you see patterns — certain groups consistently receiving lower scores, fewer recommendations, or different treatment — document it and escalate to your administration and the vendor.
🎛️
User Control
+
User control refers to the degree to which educators, students, and families can influence how an AI tool operates, what data it uses, and what outputs it produces. A high-control AI tool allows educators to adjust settings, override recommendations, opt specific students out, review and delete data, and customize how the AI engages with different learners. A low-control tool operates as a black box — you take what it gives you with little ability to intervene. In education, user control is a direct reflection of how much the tool respects educator professional judgment.
Control questions to ask any vendor:
Can educators override or adjust AI recommendations?
Can specific students be opted out of AI features while still using the platform?
Can families view, correct, or delete their child's data?
Prioritize tools that give educators and families genuine control. Avoid tools that require you to accept all outputs without the ability to review, override, or customize — those tools have made you a passenger rather than a professional.
🤝
Stakeholder Consultation
+
Stakeholder consultation means meaningfully involving the people most affected by an AI tool — students, families, teachers, support staff, and community members — in the decision-making process before and after adoption. It's the opposite of top-down technology mandates where tools are rolled out to students without input from the educators who will use them or the families whose children will be affected. Genuine consultation isn't a checkbox; it's an ongoing dialogue that ensures diverse perspectives shape how technology is used in schools.
Questions for meaningful consultation:
Have teachers been included in piloting and evaluating this tool before district-wide adoption?
Have families — especially those from marginalized communities — been given a genuine voice in this decision?
Are student perspectives being sought and considered?
Advocate for educator inclusion in any AI adoption process at your school or district. If tools are being rolled out without teacher input, name that gap and ask for it to be addressed — your professional knowledge of your students is essential to making good technology decisions.
🔄
Continuous Review
+
Continuous review is the ongoing process of evaluating an AI tool's performance, impact, and appropriateness — not just before adoption, but throughout its entire time in use. AI tools evolve, their terms of service change, new research on their limitations emerges, and the needs of a student population shift. A tool that was appropriate and effective in one semester may become problematic the next. Responsible AI use in schools requires scheduled, documented review cycles — not the assumption that a vetted tool stays vetted forever.
Questions for your ongoing review process:
Have the tool's terms of service or data policies changed since we adopted it?
Are we seeing equitable outcomes across all student groups, semester over semester?
What do teachers, students, and families say about how this tool is working in practice?
Has new research emerged about this tool's limitations or harms?
Schedule a formal review of every AI tool in use at the end of each semester. Document findings, involve stakeholders, and be willing to discontinue a tool that no longer meets your standards — even if it was once a good choice.

📌 Eight Concepts to Remember
Risk/Benefit Analysis — Weigh benefits against harms before adopting. Document it. Revisit it after use begins.
Privacy vs. Convenience — Free tools often have a real cost in student data. Scrutinize the trade-off before accepting it.
Data Handling — Know what's collected, how it's stored, and how to get it deleted. Vague policies are a warning sign.
Compliance — FERPA, COPPA, and state privacy laws are the legal floor. Compliance is necessary but not sufficient for ethical use.
Bias and Fairness Testing — Ask vendors for equity testing results, then monitor your own student population for disparate outcomes.
User Control — Tools that don't let you override, opt out, or customize make you a passenger. Insist on genuine educator control.
Stakeholder Consultation — Teachers, families, and students should have a genuine voice in AI adoption decisions — not just a notification after the fact.
Continuous Review — Vetted once doesn't mean vetted forever. Schedule regular equity and performance reviews for every AI tool in use.
