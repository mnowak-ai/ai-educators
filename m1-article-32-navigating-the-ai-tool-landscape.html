Module 1 · AI in Education

Navigating the AI Tool Landscape

There are hundreds of AI tools competing for your attention. This section helps you cut through the noise — choosing tools that genuinely serve your students, protect privacy, and keep you in control.

Before You Try Any AI Tool

The eight concepts in this section give you a framework for evaluating any AI tool — whether it's pitched at a conference, recommended by a colleague, or pushed by your district. Use this quick checklist to see how ready you are to evaluate a tool critically:

🔍 Tool Evaluation Readiness Checklist
Check off everything you know how to ask before adopting a new AI tool. See how many you can check:
I can explain what specific problem this tool solves for my students or my practice.
I know whether this tool has been approved for student data privacy by my district.
I can describe how the tool's AI makes decisions and what data it uses.
I've considered whether all my students can access this tool — including those with disabilities or limited devices.
I know what I'll do if the tool makes an error or produces biased output.
I've thought about what my role is while the AI is assisting — not just after it outputs something.
I've tested the tool myself before introducing it to students.
I'm comfortable explaining to a parent or administrator exactly how this tool is being used.

Purpose-Driven Tool Use
+
Choosing and using AI tools based on a clearly defined instructional need — not novelty, trend, or pressure. Purpose-driven educators start with a problem ("My students struggle to get feedback quickly enough") and then find tools that solve it. They don't start with a tool and invent a justification. This mindset keeps students at the center and prevents adoption fatigue.
What specific problem does this tool solve for my students or my practice — and is this the best way to solve it?
Shiny object syndrome — adopting tools because they're new or hyped, without a clear connection to learning goals. Every new tool adds cognitive load for you and your students.
🔍
Transparency
+
The degree to which an AI tool makes its processes, data sources, and decision-making visible and understandable to users. A transparent AI tool can explain (at least broadly) how it generates its outputs, what data it was trained on, and what its limitations are. Transparency matters in education because educators are accountable for the outputs — they need to understand the tool well enough to catch errors and explain its use to students and families.
Can I explain to a parent or student how this tool works and where its outputs come from? Does the company publish documentation about its AI's limitations?
Black-box tools that give you outputs with no explanation. If you can't understand why a tool recommended something, you can't responsibly act on it — especially with students.
🔒
Data Privacy
+
The protection of student and educator personal data when using AI tools. In the U.S., student data privacy is governed by laws like FERPA (for education records) and COPPA (for children under 13). Educators have a legal and ethical obligation to ensure that any AI tool used with students does not collect, store, or share data in ways that violate those protections — even if the tool is free and easy to use.
Has this tool been reviewed and approved by my district for student data privacy? Does the tool store student inputs, and if so, how and for how long?
Free consumer AI tools that haven't been vetted for educational use. Many popular public tools store user inputs to train their models — meaning student work and information could be used in ways families haven't consented to.
✋
Usability (Ease of Use)
+
How intuitive, accessible, and practical a tool is for real educators and students in real classroom conditions. A powerful AI tool that requires extensive training, unreliable internet, or expensive hardware is not actually usable for most teachers. Usability considers the full context — the devices available in your school, the technical confidence of your students, and the time you realistically have to learn a new system.
Can I learn this tool in under an hour? Will my students be able to use it without needing significant technical support? Does it work on the devices and bandwidth available in my classroom?
Tools that look great in a demo but require high technical skill or optimal conditions to function. If a tool creates more friction than it removes, it's not serving your students.
🔑
Educator Agency
+
The principle that teachers maintain professional authority over all AI-assisted decisions. When evaluating tools, educator agency means asking: does this tool put me in control, or does it gradually shift decision-making to the algorithm? Tools that support agency give educators the ability to override, adjust, or reject AI suggestions. Tools that undermine agency make educators feel like operators of a system rather than professionals exercising judgment.
Does this tool support my professional judgment, or does it make it harder to override or question its outputs? Who is ultimately accountable for what this tool produces?
Tools designed to automate educator decisions without meaningful human review. If a tool is making recommendations about students that you feel pressured to accept without questioning, your agency is being eroded.
🛩️
Co-Pilot Approach
+
A model for AI use where the educator remains firmly in the pilot seat — directing the work, setting the destination, and making all final calls — while AI acts as a capable co-pilot that handles routine tasks and surfaces information. The co-pilot approach explicitly rejects the idea of AI "flying the plane" autonomously in an educational setting. It's a mental model for healthy, agentic AI integration: you lead, AI assists.
Am I using this tool as an assistant that supports my decisions — or has it started making decisions I'm just approving automatically?
Automation creep — the gradual slide from reviewing AI outputs to rubber-stamping them. The co-pilot becomes the pilot without anyone noticing. Regular reflection on your actual usage patterns is essential.
⚖️
Equity and Accessibility
+
The principle that AI tools must work for all students — regardless of disability status, language background, device access, or socioeconomic context. Equity in AI tool selection means evaluating whether a tool creates or exacerbates gaps. Accessibility means ensuring that students who use assistive technology, have limited internet, or are learning in a second language can meaningfully engage with the tool.
Does this tool work for my students with IEPs or 504s? Does it support multiple languages? Can students access it on older devices or with limited bandwidth?
AI tools that were designed primarily for well-resourced, English-speaking, neurotypical users. Using inequitable tools in a diverse classroom can unintentionally reinforce the same disparities educators are working to address.
🧪
Critical Evaluation
+
The ongoing practice of examining AI tools and their outputs with a questioning, evidence-based mindset — rather than accepting them at face value. Critical evaluation means testing tools before deployment, reviewing AI-generated outputs before sharing them, questioning the assumptions built into a tool's design, and regularly asking whether a tool is still serving its original purpose. It's a professional habit, not a one-time event.
Have I tested this tool's outputs for accuracy, bias, and appropriateness before using them with students? Am I still evaluating it regularly, or did I stop after the first month?
The "set it and forget it" trap — adopting a tool, integrating it into your workflow, and then never revisiting whether it's still accurate, appropriate, or better than alternatives that have emerged since.

The best tool is never the most powerful one — it's the one that solves your specific problem without creating new ones.

📌 Eight Lenses for Evaluating Any AI Tool
Purpose-Driven — Start with a clear instructional problem, then find a tool. Not the other way around.
Transparency — You should be able to explain how a tool works and defend its outputs to anyone who asks.
Data Privacy — Never use an unvetted tool with student data. FERPA compliance is non-negotiable.
Usability — If it doesn't work in real classroom conditions, it doesn't work. Full stop.
Educator Agency — You must always be able to override, question, and reject any AI suggestion.
Co-Pilot Approach — You're the pilot. AI assists. Watch for automation creep turning that around.
Equity & Accessibility — If a tool doesn't work for all your students, it doesn't belong in your classroom.
Critical Evaluation — Adopt tools carefully, test them continuously, and never stop asking whether they're still working.
