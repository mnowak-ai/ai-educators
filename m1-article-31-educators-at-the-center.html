Module 1 · AI in Education

Educators at the Center

AI is reshaping — not replacing — the work of educators. These seven concepts capture what it means to be a teacher in an AI-enhanced classroom, and why your role matters more than ever.

Each term below comes with two layers: how AI fits into that concept, and what remains irreplacably human. Click any pill to jump directly to a term, or read through from top to bottom.

Learning Designer
+
A Learning Designer is an educator who shifts from simply delivering content to intentionally crafting meaningful learning experiences. Rather than asking "What will I teach today?", a Learning Designer asks "How will students learn, make meaning, and grow today?" This shift places the teacher as the architect of engagement — choosing the right tools, sequences, and experiences for their specific students.
AI can generate first drafts of lesson frameworks, suggest activities aligned to standards, or provide multiple versions of a task at different complexity levels — dramatically cutting prep time.
Only you know your students' interests, prior experiences, what motivates them, and what context will make a lesson land. The design decisions that actually make learning stick come from you.
🎯
Differentiation
+
Differentiation is the practice of adjusting instruction, content, process, or product to meet the diverse needs of learners in the same classroom. It recognizes that students learn at different rates, have different strengths, and need different levels of support or challenge to thrive. Effective differentiation is one of the most powerful — and most time-intensive — things a teacher can do.
AI can rapidly generate multiple versions of the same reading or task at different complexity levels, create tiered practice sets, or suggest scaffold strategies — making true differentiation feasible at scale without hours of extra prep.
Only you can observe in real time which students are struggling, which are coasting, and which need a different kind of support entirely. Differentiation is a judgment call, not an algorithm.
🤝
Professional Learning Community (PLC)
+
A Professional Learning Community is a group of educators who meet regularly to collaborate, share data, and improve their practice together. PLCs are built on the principle that collective inquiry — teachers analyzing student work, identifying patterns, and testing instructional strategies together — leads to better outcomes than any individual teacher working in isolation.
AI can summarize student performance data, generate potential discussion prompts for PLC meetings, draft action item lists, or synthesize trends across a data set — reducing the logistics burden so teams can spend more time on professional dialogue.
The human connection, trust, and shared accountability at the heart of a PLC can't be automated. Challenging each other, supporting each other, and making collective commitments to students requires people — not tools.
👤
Personalization
+
Personalization in education means tailoring learning experiences to the individual — their goals, strengths, interests, pace, and background. It goes beyond differentiation (which responds to varying skill levels) to honor the whole learner. True personalization asks: what does this specific student need to grow, feel seen, and become who they're capable of becoming?
AI can adapt content based on student performance data, recommend next steps based on what a student has mastered, or generate practice aligned to a student's current gaps — making data-driven personalization far more scalable.
Knowing a student's home language, their confidence level, what makes them light up, and what shuts them down — that knowledge comes from relationship. Personalization at its deepest level is a human act.
⚙️
Automation (in Education)
+
Automation in education refers to using technology to complete routine, repeatable tasks without direct human effort each time. In a classroom context, this includes things like auto-grading multiple-choice quizzes, generating progress reports, sending reminder communications, or populating rubric scores. Done well, automation returns hours to educators each week — time that can be reinvested in teaching, relationships, and creativity.
AI can automate quiz grading, generate first-draft progress report comments, send scheduled parent updates, or populate attendance patterns — handling the repeatable so teachers don't have to.
No automated system should ever replace human review before anything reaches a student or family. And the tasks that require professional judgment — assessing student growth over time, handling sensitive situations, reading the room — will always require a person.
🏛️
Collaborative Leadership
+
Collaborative Leadership in an education context means that teachers don't just follow institutional decisions about AI — they help shape them. Educators are at the front line of how AI tools affect students, and their voice in school-wide policy, tool selection, and implementation decisions is essential. Collaborative leaders advocate for students, share what's working, raise concerns, and help colleagues navigate new terrain together.
AI can help educators prepare for leadership conversations — organizing data, drafting talking points, or summarizing research on best practices — so teacher voices are backed by evidence when they enter decision-making spaces.
Leadership is inherently human. Trust, influence, advocacy, and the ability to inspire colleagues require people who are present, credible, and invested in each other's success — not tools that optimize.
🔑
Educator Agency
+
Educator Agency is the capacity and authority of teachers to make professional decisions about their practice — including decisions about when, how, and whether to use AI tools. It means teachers are not passive recipients of technology handed down from above, but active professionals who evaluate tools critically, set their own boundaries, and advocate for approaches that serve students well. In an AI age, educator agency is not optional — it's essential.
AI can support agency by giving educators faster access to information, reducing administrative burden, and freeing up time for the reflective practice that strong professional judgment requires.
Agency itself is inalienably human. No tool gives you agency — you exercise it. Your professional voice, your critical judgment, and your commitment to your students are what keep AI in its proper place: as a support, not a decision-maker.

AI offers tools that amplify your impact — but only you can decide how, when, and why to use them.

📌 Seven Concepts to Remember
Learning Designer — Teachers architect meaningful experiences; AI handles the first-draft logistics so you can focus on what makes learning stick.
Differentiation — AI makes multiple versions of content scalable; teachers make the real-time judgment calls about who needs what.
PLC — AI reduces logistics burden in collaborative planning; the trust and accountability between educators remains human.
Personalization — AI adapts based on data; knowing the whole student — their confidence, background, and goals — comes from relationship.
Automation — AI handles repeatable tasks; human review is always required before anything reaches students or families.
Collaborative Leadership — Teachers shape AI policy in schools; advocacy and influence require human credibility and presence.
Educator Agency — You decide how, when, and whether to use AI. Professional judgment belongs to you — not the tool.
